## What Is This?

This subtopic provides guidance on how to position the RWTS (Reduced Work Time Saved) metric when discussing AI value with customers. It frames RWTS as a standardized, comparative estimate rather than a guaranteed time-savings figure, and sets out implementation considerations to ensure stakeholders interpret the metric correctly. It fits within the Measure - Value practice as guidance for executive reporting and value communication.

---

## How to Position RWTS with Customers

When discussing RWTS with customers, position the metric as:

> "A standardized estimate of human effort avoided, used to compare AI value consistently across skills and use cases—not a promise of exact minutes saved for every user."

It is also important to reinforce that:

- RWTS is designed for comparative and trending analysis
- AICT value reporting emphasizes consistency over exact precision
- Customers may contextualize or refine assumptions using their own operational benchmarks for executive ROI discussions

---

## Implementation Considerations

- Ensure stakeholders understand that RWTS is an estimated value metric, not a direct productivity audit
- Use RWTS primarily for trend analysis, adoption reporting, and portfolio-level value measurement
- Align executive reporting with the directional nature of the metric
- Avoid positioning RWTS as a contractual or guaranteed time-savings claim
- Supplement RWTS with customer-specific KPIs where higher precision is required

---

## How to Use This in Implementation

When communicating AI value to customers and executives, implementers should present RWTS as a standardized, comparative estimate of human effort avoided rather than a precise per-user time savings. Use it for trend analysis, adoption reporting, and portfolio-level value measurement, and align executive ROI discussions with the metric's directional nature. Do not present RWTS as a contractual or guaranteed claim; where higher precision is needed, supplement it with customer-specific KPIs and the customer's own operational benchmarks.
