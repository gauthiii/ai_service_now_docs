# AI Governance Life Cycle

## Overview

The **AI governance life cycle** describes how organizations govern AI from initial demand through deployment and ongoing monitoring by using the **AI Control Tower (AICT)** and **AI Risk and Compliance (AIRC)** applications together. Governing AI is a continuous process that spans the entire life cycle of an AI asset — from initial demand and intake through deployment, monitoring, and value realization.

The two applications support a coordinated approach to enterprise AI governance:

- **AICT** provides centralized visibility and life cycle state management for AI initiatives across the organization. It manages the AI life cycle and serves as the **system of record**.
- **AIRC** delivers risk, regulatory, and ethical governance capabilities that help organizations evaluate whether AI systems are developed and operated responsibly and ethically. It performs **independent risk, regulatory, and control governance**.

When a new AI system is introduced — or an existing system is significantly updated and registered for governance — it enters a **governed life cycle**. This life cycle spans **intake, assessment, build, review, deployment, and monitoring**. Governance activities across these stages help organizations understand risk, apply appropriate controls, and make informed deployment decisions.

## How AI Assets Move Through the Life Cycle

| Stage | What happens |
|-------|--------------|
| **Intake / Onboard** | New AI use cases, enhancements, and ideas are submitted for governance. A centralized entry point captures purpose, scope, intended outcomes, and usage context. |
| **Assessment** | AI systems undergo impact and risk assessments to evaluate regulatory exposure, ethical considerations, and operational risk. Outcomes inform risk classification, required controls, and review routing. |
| **Development (Build & Test)** | Governance focuses on control attestation, issue management, and policy exception handling from a risk and compliance perspective. |
| **Pre-deployment Review** | AI systems are reviewed to confirm required assessments are complete. Open issues and policy exceptions must be resolved or formally accepted, and residual risk posture must be understood. The system may be approved for deployment, returned for remediation, or blocked from release. |
| **Operational Monitoring** | After deployment, ongoing monitoring maintains visibility into emerging risks, changes in usage, and evolving compliance requirements. |
| **Value Realization** | Adoption, performance, and outcomes are measured to determine whether AI initiatives deliver expected value. |
| **Life Cycle Closure (Retire / Offboard)** | Assets are formally retired, documented, and removed from active governance obligations. |

Significant changes or incidents may require governance activities to **reenter earlier life-cycle phases**.

### Benefits of consistent governance across the life cycle

- Centralize visibility into AI use and align AI initiatives with business strategy.
- Assess and manage risk for both **in-house and third-party AI**.
- Support scalable deployment with continuous oversight.
- Enable consistent oversight across all life cycle processes, from demand through value realization and life cycle closure.

## How AICT and AIRC Work Together

- **AICT** manages the AI life cycle and serves as the system of record. (See *AI Control Tower Home*, *AI asset lifecycle*, *Exploring AI Control Tower*.)
- **AIRC** performs independent risk, regulatory, and control governance.
- **Life cycle progression in AICT depends on risk and compliance decisions made in AIRC** (shown in the *Risk and compliance tab in AI Control Tower*).
- Neither application replaces the other; together they form a coordinated governance framework.

> **Note:** The AI governance life cycle is supported by a set of **life-cycle tasks** executed across AICT and AIRC. These tasks provide **operational checkpoints** that help ensure governance, risk, and compliance obligations are addressed at the appropriate stage of the life cycle.

## AI Governance Roles and Responsibilities

The life cycle involves distinct roles with clearly separated responsibilities across AICT and AIRC.

| Role | Technical name | Responsibilities |
|------|----------------|------------------|
| **AI asset owners** (also called product owners) | `sn_ai_asset_mgmt.ai_asset_owner` | Accountable for AI systems, models, and datasets as AI assets. Initiate intake, provide business context during assessment, authorize deployment and retirement decisions (subject to risk/compliance approval), and own outcomes such as value realization and life cycle closure. |
| **AI Risk and Compliance managers** | `sn_grc_ai_gov.ai_risk_and_compliance_manager` | Provide independent risk and regulatory oversight. Initiate and oversee impact and risk assessments, determine risk classification, validate control implementation, and approve or block life cycle progression. |
| **AI Risk and Compliance business users** | `sn_grc_ai_gov.ai_risk_and_compliance_business_user` | Execute governance activities — contribute assessment inputs, perform tasks such as control attestations, support remediation and issue management, and keep inventory records accurate. |
| **AI stewards** | `sn_ai_governance_ai_steward` | Same execution-level governance activities; also initiate/manage the assessment phase and keep inventory accurate. |
| **Other governance stakeholders** (security, legal, privacy, data governance teams) | — | Participate as needed, contributing expertise during assessment, review, and investigation. **Do not own life cycle decisions.** |

## Life Cycle Phases and Processes

These phases describe AI governance activities at a conceptual level and may span multiple workflow steps within AICT and AIRC.

### 1. Demand: Intake or Onboard

The life cycle begins with **intake**, establishing a centralized entry point to capture purpose, scope, intended outcomes, and usage context. This provides early visibility into AI demand, enabling identification of potential risk, duplication, or misalignment before development begins.

- Any team member with the **AI asset owner**, **AI steward**, or **AIRC business** role can submit intake-related information using the **Employee Center**.
- AI asset owners or AI stewards can review and progress intake requests using the **AI Control Tower Workspace** after submission.
- **Created or required during intake:** an AI intake request, an initial AI system record, and business context for the AI initiative.
- All AI use-case intake (in-house and third-party) is life-cycle-managed in **AICT**, which is required to register systems entering the governed life cycle and acts as the system of record.
- **AIRC does not own intake**; it participates after intake by contributing risk/compliance context (impact assessments, regulatory evaluation) once a use case is registered in AICT.
- Enhancement requests, ideas, and feedback can be managed if **Strategic Portfolio Management** is installed, but these **do not enter the AI governance life cycle** through AICT or AIRC.

> **Note:** An intake request is a *proposal* to introduce or govern an AI use case. Submitting one does **not** create or deploy an AI asset. After intake review, AI systems, models, and datasets can be registered in AICT. Categories selected at intake (such as **Agentic** or **Generative**) provide **descriptive context only** — life cycle progression, assessment requirements, and governance decisions are driven by risk classification, assessment outcomes, and configured governance rules, not by category selection.

#### AI Asset Inventory and Discovery

A life-cycle-wide capability beginning in the Demand phase and persisting across all stages.

- AI systems, models, datasets, prompts, and related artifacts are registered or discovered and recorded in a **centralized AI inventory**.
- Provides an authoritative view of AI assets enterprise-wide, including internally developed and third-party AI.
- Inventory records persist from intake through deployment and retirement, enabling traceability and governance.
- Assets are added via **manual registration** (during intake/onboarding) and **automated discovery** using configured integrations with supported cloud and third-party platforms. Discovered assets/relationships are reviewed and completed by AI asset owners and AI stewards.
- **AICT owns and manages the inventory** (system of record for asset records, relationships, and life cycle state). **AIRC consumes inventory data** for impact assessments, risk classification, control evaluation, and regulatory reporting, but does not create or own inventory records.

> **Note:** Automated discovery requires configuration of supported integrations.

### 2. Build and Validate: Assess

Governance activities are executed through **life cycle playbooks** attached to AI asset records in AICT. Playbooks define required assessment tasks, approvals, and handoffs across governance roles and coordinate tasks across AICT and AIRC.

Playbooks guide the following roles through assessment:

| Role | Activity in the Assess phase |
|------|------------------------------|
| **AI stewards** | Initiate and manage the assessment phase in AICT — start life cycle reviews, assign playbook tasks, monitor progression. |
| **AI asset owners** | Provide system details and business context; complete required assessments (impact/risk) using AI inventory data. |
| **AIRC analysts** | Perform governance evaluations in AIRC — regulatory impact assessments, risk classification, identification of required controls. |
| **AIRC managers** | Review assessment outcomes, determine whether risks are acceptable, and approve required controls/mitigations before the system advances. |

- AICT coordinates task execution, approvals, and status transitions between AICT and AIRC.
- **Created or updated:** impact assessment results, risk classification, identified risks, and recommended controls. These determine whether the system can proceed.

### 3. Build and Validate: Build and Test

Applies governance during development by ensuring risks identified during assessment are addressed through controls, remediation, and validation.

- Playbooks continue to guide remediation, control attestation, and evidence collection; progression depends on completion of required governance tasks.
- **AICT** manages life cycle state and tracks governance progress. **AIRC** validates that required controls are implemented and that issues/exceptions are documented and addressed.
- AI asset owners and development teams implement controls and remediation. AIRC business users and analysts review control attestations and evidence. AIRC managers verify controls and remediation are implemented and effective.
- **Control attestations may occur at multiple stages** (assessment, build and validation, offboarding). Completing attestations contributes to governance readiness but **does not on its own approve deployment**.
- **Created or updated during validation:** control attestations, issue records, remediation evidence, and exception records.

### 4. Build and Validate: Pre-deployment Review

Before deployment, AI systems undergo review to confirm required assessments are complete, high-priority issues and exceptions are addressed, and residual risk is understood and documented.

- **AICT** manages the pre-deployment life cycle gate. **AIRC** performs a final governance review of deployment readiness based on risk posture, control implementation, and residual risk.
- Playbooks **show** governance readiness (assessment completion, control status, open issues, documented residual risk) but **do not approve deployment**.
- **AIRC managers approve or block deployment.** Governance decisions are made in AIRC; life cycle state changes are recorded and enforced in AICT.

### 5. Deploy, Monitor, and Assess Value: Monitor

After deployment, governance activities monitor AI systems for reported events, operational changes, and governance signals.

- Supports effective risk management and responsiveness through assessments, reviews, and regulatory updates; maintains an auditable governance posture.
- Ongoing monitoring may generate governance events requiring investigation or formal decision-making.
- **AI Case Management** is a cross-life-cycle capability in AIRC that supports **investigation, exception handling, and regulatory response** without changing the AI system's life cycle phase unless governance decisions require it.
- **AICT** tracks life cycle state and operational signals. **AIRC** uses monitoring outputs to identify when reassessment, control updates, or intervention is required.
- **AIRC managers** determine whether changes, reassessments, or intervention are required. Cases and inquiries are a normal part of ongoing governance and **do not necessarily indicate failure or non-compliance**.

### 6. Deploy, Monitor, and Assess Value: Value

Focuses on measuring adoption, performance, and outcomes to understand whether AI initiatives deliver expected business value and strategic impact.

- **AICT** captures value, adoption, and outcome metrics, associating them with AI systems and use cases.
- **AI asset owners** decide whether to sustain, expand, adjust, or retire the system based on measured outcomes; **AIRC managers** confirm ongoing risk remains acceptable.
- Value measurement informs ongoing risk acceptance and helps justify continued exposure to AI-related risk.
- Value outcomes captured in AICT can be made available to **Strategic Portfolio Management** to inform portfolio planning, funding, and prioritization.
- Governance actions identified during monitoring may result in reassessment, additional controls, or life cycle state updates managed through AICT.

### 7. Life Cycle Closure: Retire and Offboard

AI systems, models, and datasets may be retired or offboarded when they no longer meet business needs, introduce unacceptable risk, or don't deliver expected value.

- Closure reduces residual risk by ensuring assets are formally retired, documented, and no longer subject to active governance obligations.
- **AICT** manages closure and updates the AI system life cycle state. **AIRC** validates that final governance obligations are met and no outstanding risk/compliance requirements remain.
- **AI asset owners, with confirmation from AIRC managers, authorize** retirement/offboarding.
- **Created or updated during offboarding:** updated life cycle state, retirement justification, and final governance record.

> **Note:** Features described in this life cycle may require additional configurations, supporting applications, or content packs. Availability and behavior depend on installed products, assigned roles, and configuration.

## Intake Requests

Intake requests are the **entry point** for managing and governing AI initiatives. They capture essential information about proposed AI systems, models, and datasets so requests can be reviewed, triaged, and evaluated early in the life cycle.

### Overview

- Capture AI-related needs and concerns in a structured way — what AI is being proposed, how it's intended to be used, and what risk/compliance considerations apply.
- Support creation of AI asset records tracked in AICT and evaluated using AIRC workflows.
- Can support **early screening and initial risk-based classification**, but do **not** automatically approve or govern an AI asset.

### Intake request types

| Request type | Purpose |
|--------------|---------|
| **Request an AI use case** | Propose a new or changed AI system addressing a business problem. Focuses on overall purpose, scope, and intended outcome. |
| **Request an AI model** | Used when the primary focus is an AI model rather than a complete system — proposing development, procurement, or onboarding of a specific model. Captures model-specific details (intended usage, versioning, risk considerations). |
| **Request a dataset** | Request access to a dataset for analysis, model training, or evaluation. Ensures data usage is reviewed before use in AI activities. |

### Risk-Based Classification at Intake

After upgrading to **version 22.0.3**, users with the **AI risk and compliance user** (`sn_grc_ai_gov.ai_risk_and_compliance_business_user`) role can support configurable risk-based classification of AI systems at intake where enabled.

- Provides an **initial screening** based on how AI systems are intended to be used; helps determine the level of governance attention required.
- Classification is derived from responses to screening questions in the **Use and purpose** section of the intake form and evaluated using a configured **Risk Assessment Methodology (RAM)**.
- Resulting classification: **Low, Medium, High, Unacceptable, or To Be Determined** — recorded on the AI system to guide downstream workflows.
- Classification is **re-evaluated automatically** when Use and Purpose responses change (e.g., changes to data sensitivity, system autonomy, level of human involvement). Changes are captured in the activity history for audit purposes.
- The **Use and Purpose** section is automatically enabled when risk-based classification at intake is configured — delivered as part of the product, no manual form customization needed.

#### Required configuration (by AI Risk and Compliance Admin)

Risk-based classification at intake occurs only when an admin (`sn_grc_ai_gov.ai_risk_and_compliance_admin`) completes all required steps:

- Set the **Migrate to Advanced Risk Assessments** property to publish Risk Assessment Methodologies (RAMs).
- **Publish assessment templates.**
- Configure `sn_grc_ai_gov.ai_system_automated_risk_classification_asmt_ram` to specify a default RAM for automated regulatory risk classification at intake.
- Configure `sn_grc_ai_gov.aisystem_primary_ram` to specify a default RAM for AI systems.

> If prerequisites aren't met, intake submissions are accepted but **risk classification isn't performed** and defaults to **To Be Determined**. After the asset is managed by an AI Steward, Automated Risk Classification pre-classifies it as High/Medium/Low/Unacceptable.

> **Note:** Risk-based classification at intake provides early screening only. Risk Rating is evaluated only for AI system requests explicitly **Managed by an AI steward** (`sn_ai_governance.ai_steward`). It does **not** approve deployment, initiate life-cycle workflows, or replace impact assessments, detailed risk assessments, or control evaluations.

### Intake Example: Email Assistant AI Agent for Corporate Communications

A corporate IT team proposes an **Email Assistant AI Agent** to help internal users draft, summarize, and respond to work emails (generating suggested responses, summarizing threads, highlighting required actions).

A team member (e.g., AI Product Owner, Use Case Submitter, or Product Innovator) submits a **Request an AI use case** intake through the Employee Center, capturing:

- **Business purpose** — improving response time, reducing manual effort.
- **Intended users** — employees using corporate email systems.
- **Types of data processed** — internal emails that may contain business-sensitive or confidential information.
- **Level of automation** — suggestions only vs. automatic sending.
- **Role of human oversight** — employee review/approval before sending.

Based on the **Use and purpose** responses, the workflow performs an initial risk-based classification (factors: data sensitivity, exposure to personal/confidential info, degree of human involvement). The resulting classification is recorded on the AI system and determines next steps (additional risk assessments, legal review, security evaluations).

## AI Cases and Inquiries

AI cases and inquiries provide a structured way to report, track, and manage AI-related concerns and questions as part of AI governance in AICT and AIRC.

### Overview

- The **AI Case Management** application is used as part of AIRC. It provides workflows to review, investigate, and respond to AI-related risks, incidents, and governance questions associated with assets governed in AICT.
- Helps identify potential issues, capture governance signals, and ensure AI systems align with organizational policies, regulatory expectations, and ethical principles.
- Viewable from the **AI Risk and Compliance dashboard** (AI cases tab and Inquiries tab).

### AI Cases vs. AI Inquiries

| Type | Use |
|------|-----|
| **AI cases** | Report observed AI-related concerns, risks, incidents, or potential noncompliance. |
| **AI inquiries** | Ask questions or request guidance about AI systems, acceptable usage, governance requirements, or next steps. |

> **Note:** The distinction may vary by organization and configuration. Both follow a structured review and management process once submitted.

### Submission and Intake

- **AI Case Business Users** (`sn_ai_case_mgmt.ai_case_business_user`) submit cases and inquiries through the **Employee Center**.
- Submissions include the AI system/model/dataset involved, observed behavior or concern, impacted users/business areas, and supporting details/attachments.

#### Anonymous submission (version 22.0.3+)

After upgrading to **22.0.3** and installing the AI Case Management application, organizations can enable **anonymous submission** of AI cases through the Employee Center — supporting broader participation while protecting reporter identity.

- When the application is installed, the **Anonymous Report Center** record producers and plugin are installed and enabled by default.
- Before an anonymous report is saved, all free-text fields (case type, summary, business unit, jurisdiction) are **sanitized** to:
  - Remove HTML and embedded script/style content
  - Restrict input to standard alphanumeric characters and basic punctuation
  - Trim extra whitespaces
- Sanitization reduces the risk of unsafe or malformed input.

### Case Management and Follow-up

- After submission, cases and inquiries are managed in the **AI Risk and Compliance Workspace**.
- **AI Risk and Compliance analysts** (`sn_ai_case_mgmt.ai_case_analyst`) review details, investigate issues, assess impact, and work assigned tasks.
- **AI Risk and Compliance managers** (`sn_grc_ai_gov.ai_risk_and_compliance_manager`) who inherit the **AI case manager** role (`sn_ai_case_mgmt.ai_case_manager`) oversee status, prioritize work, and coordinate follow-up.
- Cases can result in tasks, identification of impacted areas (policies/regulations), and creation of related issues for remediation, escalation, or ongoing governance.

### Roles and Access

Access is controlled through roles supporting **separation of duties** and **least-privilege access**:

- **Business users** — report concerns and request guidance.
- **Analysts and managers** — investigation, assessment, coordination, oversight.
- **AI stewards** (`sn_ai_governance_ai_steward`) and other governance stakeholders (security, legal, privacy, data governance) — contribute expertise and perform review/remediation tasks as needed, **without owning business outcomes or life cycle approval decisions**.

## Assessment Templates and Risk Assessment Methodologies (RAMs)

AIRC uses **assessment templates** and **risk assessment methodologies (RAMs)** to evaluate AI assets for risk, regulatory compliance, and ethical alignment.

- **Assessment templates** define the questionnaires and evaluation criteria used during AI assessments.
- **RAMs** define the scoring frameworks and classification criteria used during risk assessments.

### Smart Assessment Engine (SAE)

SAE is the underlying assessment platform AIRC uses to create, manage, and complete all AI assessments. It provides infrastructure for assessment templates, question design, section-based organization, scoring, and collaborative workflows. AIRC uses SAE components installed with the **AI Risk and Compliance Content** application.

### Assessment Workspace

AIRC managers manage assessment templates through the **Assessment Workspace** (**Workspaces > Assessment Workspace**), which displays template name, published state, and metadata. Use it to:

- View all available templates and their published state.
- Open a template to review/edit sections, questions, and scoring logic.
- Publish draft templates to make them available for use.
- Create assessment templates for custom requirements.

> **Note:** You must publish a template before initiating assessments that use it.

### Assessment Template Structure

| Component | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Sections** | Logical groupings that organize related questions; provide structure and navigation. |
| **Questions** | Individual evaluation criteria (multiple choice, free text, rating scales, and other response types). |
| **Scoring logic** | Rules that calculate scores based on responses, determining how individual responses contribute to overall results. |
| **Risk and control mappings** | Associations between specific responses and predefined risk statements or control objectives. Drive automatic generation of governance records via Post Assessment Actions. |

### Post Assessment Actions

**Post Assessment Actions for Smart Assessments** (`sn_smart_imp_auto`) provides the automation framework that generates governance records based on assessment responses. When a user completes an assessment, the system evaluates responses against configured automation rules and creates applicable risks, controls, and other records.

- Predefined risk statements and control objectives are delivered through the **content library**, but the triggering conditions are **configurable** and must be reviewed/validated by each organization.
- Critical to the workflow: without Post Assessment Actions, responses don't automatically generate the risk statements and control objectives that drive downstream governance.
- The **sn-reusable-impact-framework** (`sn_impact_fwk`) application (installed with Post Assessment Actions) provides reusable framework components.

#### How Post Assessment Actions work (FRIA example)

When an **AI Asset Owner** (`sn_ai_asset_mgmt.ai_asset_owner`) responds to questions in a **Fundamental Rights Impact Assessment (FRIA)**, if rule criteria are satisfied:

1. The system evaluates responses against the automation rules configured for the assessment template.
2. It identifies applicable **risk statements** and **control objectives** from the content library.
3. It associates the identified risk statements and control objectives with the assessment record.
4. After the **AI Risk and Compliance analyst** (`sn_grc_ai_gov.ai_risk_and_compliance_analyst`) marks the assessment **Closed complete**, the system generates risk and control records and maps them to the AI asset.

Before records are finalized, an analyst reviews the prescribed list of generated risks and control objectives to ensure accuracy, relevance, and appropriateness.

> **Note:** Custom assessment templates require **separate configuration** of automation rules.

### Business Configuration

Configuration enables organizations to tailor assessment automation and risk evaluation behavior — typically performed by AI Risk and Compliance administrators.

#### Post-assessment actions configuration

The admin (`sn_grc_ai_gov.ai_risk_and_compliance_admin`) configures automation rules determining how assessment responses trigger association and classification of governance elements after an assessment is marked **Closed complete**.

**Supported action types:**

| Action category | Action types |
|-----------------|--------------|
| **Mapping governance elements** | Map control objectives to AI systems; Map risk statements to AI systems |
| **Area where the AI system is used** | Customer Services; External Partner Ecosystem; Finance & Accounting; HR & Workforce; Internal Operations; IT & Security; Sales & Marketing; Supply Chain |
| **Data used by the system** | Behavioral or Usage Data; Business Operational Data; Customer Interaction Data; Profile or Account Data; Public or General Info; Sensitive Business Data |
| **Intent and interaction** | Map intended outcome of the AI system; Map interaction type with end users; Map level of human involvement |
| **People affected by the AI system** | External Partners; General Customer Base; Internal Team; Public or Large Audiences |
| **Type of output produced** | Automated Decisions; Generated Content; Insight & Summaries; Ranking & Scores; Recommendations; Simple Alerts; System Actions |

These action types let organizations consistently associate governance requirements with AI systems based on assessment answers, ensuring traceability between assessment outcomes and risk/compliance artifacts.

- Base-system templates include predefined control objectives and risk statements in the content library, but the **mapping logic** is defined through post-assessment action configuration.
- Configuration is done in the **Assessment Workspace** by editing the template and defining automation rules; each rule specifies which control objectives/risk statements to associate when a response condition is met.
- After configuration, complete a **test assessment** and verify expected control objectives and risk statements are generated and mapped once marked **Closed complete**.

#### Post-assessment action configuration example

In the Assessment Workspace, an admin configures a post-assessment action on a published AI impact assessment template evaluating a privacy-related question:

- **Condition:** Response to "Does your AI system use personal data?"
- **Response:** Yes
- **Action:** Automatically map predefined governance elements to the associated AI system.

**Mapped control objectives:** Ensure Data Quality; Collect and Analyze Data; Analyze Field Data.

**Mapped risk statements:** Operational Continuity and Downtime; Unintended Consequences; Reputational Damage; Regulatory Non-compliance; Privacy Violations; Overfitting and Underfitting; Model Poisoning; Model Performance Degradation (Model Drift); Lack of Transparency and Accountability; Lack of Transparency and Explainability; Inadequate Data Protection; Failure to Address Ethical Standards; Data Security; Data Bias; Data Integrity; Algorithmic Bias and Discrimination; Data Breaches and Theft; Adversarial Attacks; Unauthorized Access to AI Models.

When the assessment is marked **Closed complete**, these mappings are evaluated and the corresponding risk and control records are automatically associated with the AI system.

#### Risk assessment methodology configuration

Administrators can configure which RAMs apply during intake, assessment, and risk evaluation. Options include specifying default RAMs for AI systems, models, and datasets, and enabling automated/advanced risk calculation.

| Property | Purpose |
|----------|---------|
| `sn_grc_ai_gov.ai_system_risk_classification_ram` | Default RAM for AI system risk classification at intake. |
| `sn_grc_ai_gov.ai_system_automated_risk_classification_asmt_ram` | Automated risk classification during intake. |
| `sn_grc_ai_gov.aisystem_primary_ram` | Default RAM used for risk assessments across AI systems. |

> **Important:** To enable **risk score roll-up** across AI assets, install the **Advanced Risk** application and set **Migrate to Advanced Risk Assessments** to **Yes**. This is a **one-way configuration change** that permanently transitions risk calculation and roll-up behavior to the Advanced Risk framework.

### Risk Assessment Methodologies (default)

RAMs define scoring frameworks, classification criteria, and contributing factors used to evaluate risks associated with AI assets. Used during intake, asset-level risk assessments, and bulk risk assessment projects. The content pack provides defaults; administrators can create custom RAMs.

| RAM | Applies to | Purpose | When used |
|-----|-----------|---------|-----------|
| **Risk classification for AI system** | AI systems | Classifies AI systems by regulatory risk level based on factors captured during intake/assessment. | During intake screening or early assessment. When applied to the use case request form, evaluates Use and Purpose responses and assigns High/Medium/Low/Unacceptable. Defaults to **To Be Determined** if admin configuration is incomplete. |
| **Automated risk classification for AI system** | AI systems | Automatically assigns an initial regulatory risk classification based on Use and Purpose responses. | During intake when automated screening is enabled. |
| **Risk assessment for AI inventory** | AI systems, models, datasets | Evaluates individual risks using likelihood, impact, and control effectiveness to calculate inherent and residual risk scores. | During asset-level and bulk risk assessment projects. Individual scores roll up to an aggregated score on the AI asset record and the Risk and Compliance dashboard. Default for bulk projects; can be set as default primary RAM via `sn_grc_ai_gov.aisystem_primary_ram`. |
| **Risk classification for AI model or dataset** | AI models, datasets | Classifies models and datasets by risk level based on characteristics, data sensitivity, and intended use. | When models/datasets require independent governance evaluation. Not applied through a global property — selected when initiating a risk assessment on a model or dataset. |

> **Important:** Automated and advanced risk scoring behavior depends on RAM configuration. To enable risk score roll-up, install Advanced Risk and migrate to Advanced Risk Assessments (one-way change).

### Automated Risk Classification (Use & Purpose + Smart Assessment + Risk Assessment)

Automated Risk Classification connects intake screening, assessments, and risk evaluation into a single governance flow. Responses provided by the Product Owner in the **Use and Purpose** section during intake are **reused across subsequent assessments** and contribute directly to classifying systems as High, Medium, or Low risk. Assessment templates, RAMs, and Post Assessment Actions work together to ensure qualitative context, regulatory impact, and quantitative risk scoring are applied consistently.

**Typical assessment sequence:**

1. **Intake** — *Automated risk classification for AI system* RAM evaluates Use and Purpose responses and assigns an initial regulatory risk classification.
2. **Assess phase** — the risk assessment captures information about potential impact on privacy, fairness, and other fundamental rights. Use and Purpose responses are carried forward to provide continuity and reduce duplicate entry.
3. **Post Assessment Actions** evaluate responses and automatically associate applicable risk statements and control objectives with the assessment record per configured rules.
4. An **AIRC analyst** reviews the prescribed list to validate accuracy, relevance, and applicability before records are finalized.
5. After **Closed complete**, the system generates risk and control records and maps them to the asset. The *Risk assessment for AI inventory* RAM then evaluates each risk using quantitative scoring to calculate **inherent and residual risk scores**.
6. For systems subject to the **EU AI Act**, additional assessments (EU AI Act Conformity Assessment, FRIA) provide specialized regulatory evaluation.

Throughout, assessment outcomes and risk scores inform decisions about whether a system can advance to the next phase.

> **Note:** Automated risk classification provides early risk context only. It does **not** approve deployment, initiate life-cycle workflows, or replace downstream impact or risk assessments.

## Offboarding AI Assets Review

Offboarding AI systems, models, and datasets ensures governance, risk, and compliance requirements are addressed throughout assessment, preparation, and retirement — including impact evaluation, residual risk management, documentation preservation, data-handling decisions, and audit traceability.

### How offboarding is handled across AIRC and AICT

- Offboarding involves both **governance activities** and **technical life cycle actions**, shared between AIRC and AICT.
- Governance review may assess how retiring an asset affects users, business processes, and dependent systems, and whether continued use, reuse, or retirement of related models/datasets is appropriate.
- Assessments during offboarding **confirm governance obligations are fully addressed before retirement** — they don't introduce new ongoing or post-retirement monitoring obligations.
- **AIRC** performs and documents governance reviews; **AICT** manages technical life-cycle workflows based on governance outcomes.
- Offboarding is **initiated** when an **AI asset owner** (`sn_ai_asset_mgmt.ai_asset_owner`) or **steward** (`sn_ai_governance_ai_steward`) submits an offboarding request in AICT, which drives governance review activities in AIRC.
- When a model/dataset is used by one or more governed systems, review may evaluate **downstream dependencies** so impacted systems are updated, remediated, or retired before the asset is fully offboarded.
- Governance activities focus on **business, legal, ethical, and safety impacts**; technical actions focus on disabling access, removing operational dependencies, and updating inventory records.
- Governance may evaluate whether legal, ethical, safety, or compliance risks remain after retirement and document how residual risks are mitigated, accepted, or formally closed.

### What is completed in AIRC during offboarding

- AIRC supports the **governance portion** — reviewing and documenting risk, impact, and compliance considerations for retiring models and datasets. When models/datasets are associated with a governed system, activities may be tracked at the **AI system level**.
- Assets and governance status are viewable on the **Operations tab** in the AIRC Workspace.
- Practitioners can review regulatory risk classifications, complete/update impact assessments, and conduct conformity or policy-alignment reviews.
- AIRC **maintains governance records** (assessments, controls, issues, attestations) that remain available after retirement, supporting **audit readiness and historical traceability**.
- Risks and issues identified during offboarding review can be tracked and formally closed in AIRC.
- **Offboarding is complete** when required governance reviews are finished, related issues are resolved or formally accepted, and the AI asset life-cycle status is updated to **retired**.

### Assessment behavior during offboarding

- Governance activities are driven by **risk classification, assessment status, and life-cycle state**. Categories selected at intake (e.g., Agentic, Generative) are descriptive metadata only and don't change assessment creation, scoring, or life-cycle behavior.
- Impact or risk assessments may be created during offboarding when review determines additional evaluation is needed (based on risk classification and existing coverage). **If an active assessment of the same type already exists, a duplicate is not created.**
- When offboarding completes and the life-cycle state is updated to **retired**, remaining open governance assessments are **closed or cancelled** per configured governance rules. Historical records remain available for audit/traceability.
- Offboarding review may confirm that documentation, assessment results, and data-handling decisions required for regulatory, audit, or investigative purposes are preserved.

### Assessment guidance during offboarding

The specific assessments considered depend on the asset's risk classification, usage context, and existing governance coverage:

| Assessment type | Use during offboarding |
|-----------------|------------------------|
| **Impact assessments** | When offboarding requires evaluating how retiring an asset affects users, business processes, downstream systems, or decision-making workflows — to determine whether retirement introduces operational, ethical, or safety impacts. |
| **Risk assessments** | To evaluate residual risks (legal, ethical, compliance) that may persist after operational use ends; to confirm previously identified risks are adequately mitigated, accepted, or formally closed. |
| **Conformity / policy-alignment reviews** | For assets subject to regulatory or internal governance requirements, to confirm applicable obligations are satisfied before offboarding is finalized. |

The creation and sequencing of offboarding-related assessment tasks are managed through **AICT**, while **AIRC** provides the governance context, assessment records, and audit traceability needed to support offboarding decisions.
