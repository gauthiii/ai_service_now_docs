Regulations that typically affect third-party risk management programs
You should consult legal and compliance experts to determine the specific regulatory landscape
relevant to your third-party relationships. Here's a list of regulations that are typically considered
when assessing third-party risk:
Anti-Money Laundering (AML) and Counter-Terrorism Financing (CTF) regulations
These regulations aim to prevent money laundering, terrorist financing, and other
illicit financial activities. They require companies to verify the identity of their third
parties, assess their sources of funds, and ensure compliance with applicable AML
and CTF laws.
Anti-Corruption and Bribery laws
Regulations such as the U.S. Foreign Corrupt Practices Act (FCPA) and the UK
Bribery Act impose strict requirements on companies to prevent bribery and
corruption. Due diligence helps identify any potential risks related to bribery or
corruption in the third party's operations and relationships.
Data Protection and Privacy regulations
With the increasing focus on data protection and privacy, regulations like the
European Union's General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) and the California
Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA) require companies to safeguard personal data. Due
diligence includes assessing a third party's data protection and privacy practices to
ensure compliance with these regulations.
Sanctions and Embargoes
Governments impose sanctions and embargoes on certain countries, individuals, or
entities to restrict trade and prevent support for illegal activities. Companies need
to ensure that their third parties aren’t subject to any sanctions or embargoes and
aren’t engaged in activities that violate these restrictions.
Financial Regulations
Depending on the industry, companies might need to consider financial regulations
such as the Sarbanes-Oxley Act (SOX) for publicly traded companies or sector-
specific regulations like the Dodd-Frank Act for financial institutions. These
regulations often require companies to assess the financial stability, reporting
accuracy, and internal controls of their third parties.
Labor and Employment Laws
Companies need to ensure that their third parties comply with labor and
employment laws, including regulations related to minimum wage, working hours,
health and safety, and equal employment opportunities. This helps mitigate risks
associated with labor violations and potential reputational harm.
Environmental Regulations
Companies might need to evaluate a third party's compliance with environmental
regulations, particularly if the third party engages in activities that have an
environmental impact. This includes assessing their environmental practices,
waste management, pollution control measures, and adherence to sustainability
standards.
Benefits of your third-party risk management program
A third-party risk management program can help you to proactively identify, assess, and mitigate
the risks that are associated with your relationships with third parties. By effectively managing
third-party risks, you can enhance your overall resilience, protect your interests, and create
sustainable and trustworthy business relationships.

Here are some key benefits and value propositions of implementing a robust third-party risk
management program:
Risk reduction and mitigation
A third-party risk management program enables you to identify and assess potential
risks associated with your third-party relationships. By conducting due diligence,
monitoring activities, and implementing risk mitigation strategies, you can reduce
the likelihood and impact of risks such as compliance violations, financial instability,
data breaches, reputation damage, operational disruptions, and legal liabilities.
Compliance and regulatory adherence
A comprehensive third-party risk management program helps you comply with
applicable laws, regulations, and industry standards. By verifying the compliance
of your third parties with various regulatory requirements, you can avoid legal
penalties, reputational damage, and potential business disruptions resulting from
non-compliance.
Enhanced reputation and brand protection
Engaging with third parties introduces risks to your reputation and brand. By
proactively managing these risks through a third-party risk management program,
you can safeguard their reputation, maintain stakeholder trust, and demonstrate
a commitment to ethical business practices and responsible supply chain
management.
Operational efficiency and continuity
Third parties such as suppliers, vendors, or service providers play critical roles
in your operations. By effectively managing third-party risks, you can validate the
reliability, performance, and continuity of your supply chain. This reduces the
likelihood of disruptions, delays, or quality issues that can impact operational
efficiency and customer satisfaction.
Cost savings and financial protection
Implementing a third-party risk management program can lead to cost savings and
financial protection. By evaluating the financial stability of third parties, you can
avoid engaging with financially vulnerable entities that might default on payments
or fail to deliver goods or services. Additionally, by helping to prevent legal and
regulatory non-compliance issues, you can avoid costly fines, penalties, legal
disputes, and associated expenses.
Enhanced decision-making and informed partnerships
A well-structured third-party risk management program provides you with
comprehensive information and insights about your third parties. This enables
better decision making when selecting and engaging with third parties, enabling
you to choose partners that align with your values, standards, and business
objectives. It also helps foster stronger and more reliable relationships based on
trust and shared risk awareness.
Strengthened cybersecurity and data protection
Third-party relationships can introduce significant cybersecurity and data
protection risks. A third-party risk management program helps assess the
cybersecurity practices and data protection measures of third parties, reducing
the chances of data breaches, unauthorized access, or data leakage. This helps
you protect sensitive information and maintain compliance with data protection
regulations.
